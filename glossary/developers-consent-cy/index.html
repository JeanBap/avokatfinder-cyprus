Developer's Consent Cyprus

Developer's consent (synainesi) in Cyprus is required before a buyer of an off-plan property can sell or mortgage the property prior to receiving a title deed, as the developer remains the registered title holder until transfer.

Why Developer Consent Is Needed

In Cyprus off-plan transactions, the developer remains the registered owner of the land and building until title deeds are issued and a formal transfer takes place. During this intervening period — which historically has lasted years — the buyer holds only a contract of sale (konvenju equivalent). If the buyer wishes to sell on, mortgage the property, or carry out major alterations, they typically require the developer's written consent (Greek: synainesi).

Mortgage Consent

Cyprus banks wishing to take a mortgage security interest against an off-plan property (where no title deed exists) need either the developer's consent to note the mortgage on the developer's title, or to lend against the contract of sale. Many developers charge a fee for consent letters and may impose conditions — for example, prohibiting the buyer from taking mortgage finance with a competing bank. Review the original purchase contract for any clauses restricting the buyer's financing options.

Sale Before Title Deed

If a buyer wishes to sell their unit before a title deed is issued, they must typically assign their contract of sale to the new buyer. This assignment requires the developer's consent in most standard Cyprus purchase contracts. Without consent, the assignment may be void or create additional liability. In practice, developers charge an assignment fee (a percentage of the new sale price) — this is negotiated and should be checked in the original contract.

Developer Insolvency Risk

If the developer becomes insolvent before consent is obtained and before a title deed is issued, the buyer's position is significantly complicated. Receiverships and liquidations in Cyprus typically freeze all company transactions, including consent processes. Law 9(I)/2015 provides some protection but enforcement is not automatic. This risk underlines the importance of buying only from financially sound developers with clear balance sheets.

Avoiding the Consent Problem

The cleanest way to avoid developer consent issues is to buy a property that already has a separate title deed in the seller's name — you then deal directly with the DLS for the transfer, without the developer involved. For off-plan purchases, negotiate the consent terms in the initial contract: specify what the developer may charge for consent, the timeframe in which they must respond, and consequences if they unreasonably withhold consent.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Signing an off-plan contract without reviewing the consent and assignment clauses. An undisclosed 5% assignment fee discovered only when you try to sell can destroy your profit margin.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Negotiate a fixed, capped developer consent fee in the purchase contract before you sign — some developers accept a flat fee (e.g. €1,000) rather than a percentage of resale price.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I refuse to get developer consent and sell anyway?

Without consent, the assignment is typically voidable under the contract — the developer may refuse to cooperate with the DLS transfer.

Does the developer's consent apply if I'm transferring to a family member?

Often yes — check the contract. Some contracts exempt gifts to spouse and children.

What if the developer refuses consent unreasonably?

If the contract does not address this, you may need to apply to court — expensive and slow. Better to negotiate consent rights upfront.
