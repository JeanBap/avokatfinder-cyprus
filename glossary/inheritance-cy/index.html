Inheritance Cyprus

Inheritance law in Cyprus governs how property passes on death, including forced heirship rules for Cypriot citizens and the EU Succession Regulation options available to foreign owners.

Cyprus Inheritance Law Overview

Cyprus inheritance law (succession law) is governed by the Wills and Succession Law (Cap. 195). For Cypriot citizens and permanent residents who die intestate (without a will), the law prescribes the distribution of assets including immovable property. Cypriot law includes forced heirship provisions — certain close relatives (spouse, children) have a statutory right to a minimum share of the estate that cannot be defeated by a will.

EU Succession Regulation for Foreign Owners

For EU citizens (including those from other EU member states) owning property in Cyprus, the EU Succession Regulation (EU 650/2012) gives individuals the option to elect for the law of their home country to govern their entire estate (including Cyprus property), rather than Cyprus law. This election must be made in a valid will. For UK nationals post-Brexit, the EU regulation no longer applies — UK nationals need specific advice on whether a Cyprus will (covering Cyprus property) or a UK will (covering global assets) or both is optimal.

Immovable Property and Probate

When a property owner dies, the Cyprus immovable property cannot be transferred to heirs until a Cyprus Grant of Probate (or Letters of Administration for intestate estates) is obtained from the Cyprus District Court. Even if the deceased had a foreign will, the Cyprus courts must recognise or re-probate it before the DLS will register the transfer of title to the heirs. This process can take several months to over a year for complex estates.

Inheritance Tax

Cyprus abolished inheritance tax in 2000. There is no estate duty or inheritance tax on Cyprus property — the estate passes to heirs tax-free (though normal income tax applies to any income earned by the estate during administration, and CGT may apply if the heirs subsequently sell the inherited property).

Planning Ahead

Foreign owners of Cyprus property should: make a Cyprus will covering Cyprus immovable property (a separate will from any home-country will); consider who the Cyprus will appoints as executor; and review joint ownership structures — property held in joint tenancy (if legally structured in Cyprus) can pass by survivorship more simply than through full probate. A Cyprus advocate and an estate-planning specialist should both be consulted.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Assuming a home-country will automatically covers Cyprus property without Cyprus probate. A foreign will must go through Cyprus recognition proceedings — expect 6–18 months' delay for estate administration.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Make a separate Cyprus will covering only your Cyprus immovable property, appointing a Cyprus-resident executor — this significantly simplifies and accelerates probate for your heirs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there a double probate risk?

Yes — if you have both a home-country will and no Cyprus will, your heirs may face probate proceedings in two jurisdictions simultaneously.

Can property pass by survivorship in Cyprus?

Cyprus recognises joint tenancy with survivorship rights if the property is properly registered as such — confirm with your advocate at the time of purchase.

Are non-EU nationals subject to the EU Succession Regulation?

No — the EU regulation only applies to EU citizens. Non-EU nationals (including UK post-Brexit) should seek specific advice.
