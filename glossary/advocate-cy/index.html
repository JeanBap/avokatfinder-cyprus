Advocate Cyprus

A Cyprus advocate (dikigoros) is a fully qualified lawyer who advises on property law, drafts contracts, conducts due diligence, and represents clients in court — a critical professional in any Cyprus property transaction.

Role of a Cyprus Advocate

In Cyprus, lawyers are called advocates (dikigoros in Greek). Cyprus operates a fused legal profession — advocates do both solicitor-type work (advising, drafting, transacting) and barrister-type work (court advocacy). In property transactions, the advocate reviews and drafts contracts, conducts title searches, advises on tax, coordinates with the DLS, and can represent the client in any dispute. The Bar Association of Cyprus (Parechomeno Dikigoros tis Kyprou) regulates the profession.

Why You Need Your Own Advocate

Unlike some civil law jurisdictions where the notary provides neutral verification, Cyprus conveyancing relies heavily on the parties' own advocates. A property buyer who uses the seller's advocate (or the developer's recommended lawyer) may not receive fully independent advice. Each party should have their own advocate — especially in cross-border transactions where the buyer may not understand local law or language.

Due Diligence the Advocate Performs

A Cyprus advocate conducting property due diligence will: search the DLS for encumbrances and title status; check planning permits at the relevant municipality; review the contract for onerous terms (consent clauses, penalty clauses, assignment fees); advise on stamp duty and CGT implications; and confirm AIP permit requirements for non-EU buyers. For complex deals, the advocate may coordinate with a tax adviser and a licensed surveyor.

Finding a Qualified Advocate

The Cyprus Bar Association maintains a public register searchable online. For foreign buyers, an advocate fluent in English (or the buyer's language) and experienced in cross-border transactions is advisable. Referrals from embassies, relocation agents, or international law firms with Cyprus desks are common starting points. Verify the advocate's current Bar registration before instructing.

Fees

Advocate fees for property conveyancing in Cyprus are not fixed by a mandatory tariff — they are market-driven. Typical fees for a residential purchase are 0.5–1.5% of the purchase price for a full service (contract review, due diligence, DLS coordination, transfer attendance). Agree the fee scope and cap in a written engagement letter before the advocate begins work.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Using the developer's recommended advocate without checking for conflicts of interest. A developer-recommended lawyer may not act independently in your interest — always appoint your own.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Brief your advocate specifically on any areas of concern — unusual terms in the contract, planning queries, or title questions. A written brief ensures you get focused advice and have a record of what was covered.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a foreign lawyer handle a Cyprus property purchase?

No — Cypriot law and DLS procedures require a Cyprus-qualified advocate for local registration and transfer work.

Can I negotiate the advocate's fee?

Yes — fees are not regulated and are negotiable, especially on higher-value transactions.

What if my advocate makes a professional error?

Advocates carry professional indemnity insurance. A negligence claim can be brought before the court or the Bar Association's disciplinary committee.
