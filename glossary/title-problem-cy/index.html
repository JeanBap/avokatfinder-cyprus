Title Problem Cyprus

The title problem in Cyprus refers to properties where buyers paid in full but cannot get a title deed because the developer mortgaged the land to banks, leaving buyers trapped without formal ownership.

Background of the Title Problem

The title problem is one of the most documented issues in Cyprus property law. From roughly the 1980s through to the 2010s, many developers sold residential units to buyers (often foreign nationals) while simultaneously using the underlying land as collateral for construction loans. When the developer completed — or even went bankrupt mid-construction — the bank's mortgage on the land blocked the Cyprus Land Registry from issuing a separate title deed to each buyer.

Scale of the Problem

At its peak, estimates suggested over 130,000 properties in Cyprus lacked title deeds for various reasons related to developer mortgages, un-split plots, or planning violations. While this number has reduced significantly through legislative intervention and DLS processing, tens of thousands of properties are still working through the system. Any buyer of a property built before approximately 2015 should specifically ask whether a clean title deed exists.

Law 9(I)/2015 and Amendments

The Cypriot government enacted Law 9(I)/2015 (the Law on Sale of Immovable Property — Specific Performance) to address the title problem. This law allows buyers with deposited contracts of sale to apply directly to the DLS for a title deed in their name, even if the developer's land has a bank mortgage — provided the buyer has paid the full contract price and the property was built as specified. The developer's creditor bank may object, but the law gives buyers a protected pathway.

What Buyers Should Do

If you are considering buying a property without a title deed, your lawyer must: verify that a contract of sale is deposited at the DLS; confirm the buyer has paid in full under the contract; establish whether any pending mortgage encumbrances can be cleared under Law 9(I)/2015 or through a direct bank settlement; and assess the timeline for obtaining a clean title. Do not buy a property without a deed unless your lawyer is satisfied that a clear pathway to obtaining one exists and has been costed.

Due Diligence Before Purchase

For any resale of a property that still lacks a title deed, confirm: the status of any DLS application under Law 9(I)/2015; whether the developer (if alive as a company) has confirmed the transfer; whether any bank that held a mortgage on the land has issued a release or consent; and whether any planning violations exist that might prevent the DLS from issuing a deed. This due diligence is specific and requires a lawyer experienced in Cyprus title defects.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a resale of a no-title-deed property without a specialist legal opinion on the pathway to obtaining the deed. Some title problems have no current solution and the property effectively cannot be transferred.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Before agreeing a price for any no-deed property, obtain a written legal opinion setting out the estimated timeline and cost for obtaining the deed — make this opinion a precondition of the offer, not something done after exchange.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a mortgage on a property without a title deed?

Some banks will lend on a deposited contract of sale, but at lower LTV and higher rates than title-deed security.

Is the title problem only in tourist areas?

No — it affects properties across Cyprus, though it is most prevalent in coastal and tourist development areas.

What is the typical timeline to resolve a title problem?

Varies enormously — from months (simple bank mortgage release) to years (developer insolvency, court proceedings).
