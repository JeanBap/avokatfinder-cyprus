Cyprus Mortgage

A Cyprus mortgage (hypothiki) is a registered security interest over immovable property, used by banks to secure home loans and subject to specific registration and discharge requirements.

What Is a Hypothiki?

In Cypriot law, a mortgage is called a hypothiki (hypothec). It is a real security interest registered against a specific property at the District Lands Office (DLS), entitling the lender (typically a bank) to priority in the sale proceeds if the borrower defaults. The borrower retains possession and use of the property throughout the loan term; only upon default does the bank's enforcement right arise.

Registration Requirement

A hypothiki must be registered at the DLS to be effective against third parties. Registration gives the bank priority over subsequently registered claims. The registration process involves submitting the mortgage deed (drafted by the bank's legal team and signed before a registered advocate) to the relevant district lands office, paying a registration fee, and obtaining official confirmation of registration. Only after this is the bank's security in place.

LTV and Lending Terms

Cypriot banks have tightened lending standards since the 2013 financial crisis. Maximum LTV for primary residence purchases is typically 80% (with some banks offering up to 90% for qualifying first-time buyers). For investment or secondary properties, LTV is generally capped at 70%. Banks also apply income stress tests — the monthly repayment must be sustainable if interest rates rise 2–3 percentage points above the contract rate.

Variable vs Fixed Rates

Cyprus mortgage rates have historically been predominantly variable, linked to the ECB base rate or the bank's own base lending rate. Fixed-rate products (typically 1–5 year fixings) are available but less common. Borrowers should understand whether their rate is ECB-linked or bank-rate-linked — the latter gives the bank more discretion to move the rate independently of ECB decisions.

Discharge (Apeleutherotiko)

When the loan is repaid, the bank must issue a release document (apeleutherotiko) and the mortgage must be formally cancelled at the DLS. This is not automatic — it requires a specific DLS application. Until the release is registered, the mortgage continues to appear on the title. Sellers must ensure the release is registered (not merely obtained from the bank) before or at the deed transfer date.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Assuming the mortgage is cleared when you make the final loan payment. Until the bank issues the apeleutherotiko and it is registered at the DLS, the hypothiki remains on the title.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: When refinancing, coordinate the discharge of the old mortgage and registration of the new one simultaneously to avoid a gap in cover that complicates the process for both lenders.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a non-resident get a Cyprus mortgage?

Yes — major Cyprus banks lend to non-residents, though at lower LTV and with stricter documentation requirements.

What happens if I default?

The bank initiates enforcement proceedings. Cyprus law was reformed post-2013 to make foreclosure faster — it now takes 1–2 years rather than the previous 5+ years.

Is mortgage interest tax deductible in Cyprus?

For corporate borrowers, yes. For individuals on primary residences, limited deductibility may apply — confirm with a Cyprus tax adviser.
