Property Survey Cyprus

A property survey in Cyprus is a professional inspection of the physical condition of a building, typically commissioned by the buyer to identify structural defects, damp, or unpermitted works before purchase.

Why a Survey Matters in Cyprus

Cyprus has no mandatory vendor disclosure requirement equivalent to a UK Property Information Form or a French dossier de diagnostics. The legal principle of caveat emptor (buyer beware) applies: it is the buyer's responsibility to inspect and satisfy themselves about the physical condition of the property before committing. A professional survey is the primary tool for discharging this duty.

Types of Survey Available

In Cyprus, buyers can commission: a Level 1 (Condition Report) — a superficial condition overview highlighting obvious defects; a Level 2 (HomeBuyer Report) — a more detailed inspection of accessible areas with a condition rating for each element; or a Level 3 (Full Building Survey / Structural Survey) — the most comprehensive, involving intrusive inspection where possible, suitable for older buildings, those with visible defects, or any property the buyer intends to significantly renovate. RICS-registered surveyors provide all three levels.

Common Issues Found in Cyprus

Cyprus buildings commonly present: rising damp (particularly in stone-built village houses); inadequate waterproofing of flat roofs (the predominant roof type); sub-standard structural reinforcement in older concrete-frame buildings; unpermitted extensions or pools; and outdated electrical and plumbing systems. A Level 2 or Level 3 survey typically surfaces these issues.

Cost and Value

Survey costs in Cyprus range from approximately €300–€400 for a Level 2 to €600–€1,200 for a comprehensive Level 3 on a complex or larger property. Given that a survey may reveal €20,000–€50,000 of remediation work, or identify an unpermitted structure that is uninsurable, the cost is almost always justified. Many buyers forgo a survey on new-build properties — but even new builds have defect risks in the first year.

Using Survey Findings to Negotiate

A survey report with costed defects is a powerful negotiation tool. In Cyprus, where the asking price is often set with margin for negotiation, presenting a surveyor's defect schedule with repair quotes can justify a price reduction or a seller-funded remediation as a condition of the sale. Sellers who refuse to negotiate on survey-evidenced defects should be treated with caution.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Skipping a survey on an 'almost new' 8-year-old resale because it 'looks well maintained'. Flat-roof waterproofing failures and damp ingress are common in 5–15-year-old Cyprus buildings and rarely visible to the untrained eye.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Always commission at least a Level 2 survey and ask the surveyor specifically to comment on flat-roof condition, pool structure (if present), and any visible additions that may be unpermitted.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I attend the survey?

Yes — most RICS surveyors in Cyprus welcome the buyer attending the end of the inspection for a verbal briefing.

Does the surveyor check planning permits?

No — a surveyor assesses physical condition; a separate permit search through your advocate covers planning compliance.

Is a survey needed for a new-build apartment?

Advisable — a snagging inspection (punch list) before accepting handover identifies defects the developer must fix at their cost.
